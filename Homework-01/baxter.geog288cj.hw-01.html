1) Review the definition of adiabatic lapse rate and static stability. A good reference is Wallace and Hobbs Atmospheric Science: an introductory survey¶

Adiabatic lapse rate: The rate of change of the temperature for a parcel of air that is being raised or lowered in the atmosphere without losing or gaining heat.

Static stability: Refers to the restoring force of the atmosphere, in which vertical mixing is inhibited by greater stability. Static stability is commonly computed as change in potential temperature over change in height.

3) Would there be a boundary layer on a planet that had an atmosphere. but that did not experience a diurnal variation of net radiation at the ground?¶

Yes, there would be a boundary layer on a planet that has an atmosphere. There will be a boundary layer as long as there is an atmosphere because there will still be a distribution of momentum and heat fluxes associated with the surface. As long as there is heat coming off of the surface or winds passing over the surface (and being affected by friction), there will be a boundary layer. Even at night on Earth there exists a boundary layer.

# Read in radiosonde data
def read_radiosonde(fname):
    names = ['Time','P','T','U','Wsp','Wdir','Altitude','Geo_Pot']
    return pd.read_csv(fname,skiprows=[0],names=names,delimiter='\t')

# Compute saturation vapor pressure
def calc_saturation_vapor_pressure(T):
    return 6.112 * np.exp((17.67 * T) / (T + 243.5))

def calc_saturation_mixing_ratio(es,P):
    es = es
    return 0.622 * es / (P - es)

# Compute water vapor mixing ratio
def calc_water_vapor_mixing_ratio(U,rs):
    return (U / 100) * rs

# Compute potential temperature (dry)
def calc_potential_temperature(T,Po,P):
    return (T + 273.15) * (Po / P) ** (2/7)

# Compute virtual potential temperature
def calc_virtual_potential_temperature(theta,r,r_l):
    #return theta * (1 + 0.61 * r - r_l)
    return theta * (r + 0.622) / (0.622 * (1 + r))

def calc(df):
    es = calc_saturation_vapor_pressure(df['T'])
    rs = calc_saturation_mixing_ratio(es,df['P'])
    r = calc_water_vapor_mixing_ratio(df['U'],rs)
    theta = calc_potential_temperature(df['T'],df['P'][0],df['P'])
    theta_v = calc_virtual_potential_temperature(theta,r,0)
    return r,theta,theta_v

4c) Discuss each profile and identify (if possible) surface layer (SL), mixed layer (ML), stable boundary layer (SBL) and free atmosphere (FA)¶

Radionsonde-A has a very well mixed layer, where potential temperature and virtual potential temperature barely changes vertically for 1.5 km. The top of the mixed layer was placed to coincide with the return to rapidly increasing potential temperature with height.

Radiosonde-B has a very stable atmospheric structure and cold surface. So the very near portion where the vertical potential temperature gradient is greatest was defined as the stable boundary layer. Where the potential temperature gradient weakens is defined as the residual layer and the free troposphere is where the gradient increases again.

Radiosonde-C has a very stable profile, similar to Radiosonde-B. This profile seems to show very little mixing, where potenital temperature just increases relatively steadily with height.

Radiosonde-D is very unstable near the surface, to the point of being super adiabatic, beneath a well mixed layer. It is difficult to place the top of the mixed layer based on potential temperature, but it may be seen by the rapid shift in the mixing ratio profile from relatively constant to quickly decreasing.

4e) Associate each sounding with early morning, noon, afternoon, late afternoon and night. Discuss and justify your answers.¶

Radiosonde-A: Afternoon. The surface is fairly warm for this profile. The surface layer is also very unstable and indicative of an afternoon profile, where the surface has been warmed by solar radiation.

Radiosonde-B: Early morning. The stability and cold surface temperatures are suggestive of a night or early morning profile. The precession of the day should start with the lowest surface potential temperature and increase over the course of the day. The winds are also very weak near the surface, possibly because of the stability. The slightly steeper near surface potential temperature gradient relative to Radiosonde-C likely indicates that this follows as an early morning profile, where the surface has cooled over night.

Radiosonde-C: Night. This profile is very stable, which likley indicates a nighttime or early morning profile. The relatively strong winds near the surface are somewhat unexpected. The profile is not quite as steep as that in Radiosonde-B, and it is possibly still releasing heat from the surface, indicating that this may be a nighttime profile.

Radiosonde-D: Late Afternoon. The rapid decrease in potential temperature with height, is indicative of instability and suggests that this may be an afternoon profile because solar radiation over the duration of the day warms the surface and the near surface air becomes more buoyant, creating turbulence.
